INSPIRATION

Tech giants like IBM, Apple, Microsoft, and Google, have a number of strategic reasons for acquiring companies—to set up the capability to expand into a particular area, acquire talent, thwart competitors, or simply gain access to those company’s patents to diversify their product offerings. It is not always clear why these companies move in a particular direction, for instance into healthcare or food distribution, so we decided to investigate.

Bringing together data from the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office as well as on seven technology company acquisitions, we built a tool to explore and visualize the connections between a tech company, it's acquisitions, and the types of intellectual property they choose to hold. The selected features were designed to better understand how these tech giants have segmented the market over time as well as anticipate how they might evolve over time based on the patents they hold and the companies they have acquired.

EXPLORATIVE ANALYSIS

Areas of exploration and the features we implemented

Tech Landscape

Exploratory tools that compare across the parent companies and identify which of them have invested in a current hot tech trend, based on the patents they own that feature these keywords (see Query 8), which years the most acquisitions happened (see Query 6a), as well as which classes of patents have been consistently renewed and the top 5 most renewed classes (see Query 7).

Acquisition Factors

To understand the nature of the acquisitions, these features explore what factors might influence a parent company's decision. For instance, their annual acquisition frequency (see Query 6b), what top 10 patent technology areas they have in common with their acquired companies (see Query 2 and Query 5), as well as their geographical proximity (see Query 4).

Focus Areas

Features that investigate where a parent company has and might continue to focus it's patent investments, by examining how their patent filing categories have changed over time (see Query 3), as well as retrieves all the patent records they and their child companies hold (see Query 1).

HOW WE DID IT

This U.S. patent and tech company acquisition exploratory tool was designed and built by a team of four students with a passion for data science and software engineering. Their combined knowledge of several technologies and programming languages brought this project to life:

Cleaning the data: our original data set was on the order of ~7.5 GB in total, containing over 10 million patent records and associated metadata. Using Python, we cleaned and trimmed this data set down to those pertaining to seven tech companies for which we had acquisition data, which reduced our file to ~200 MB and over 180,000 patent records.
Developing the database: from our data, we set up two MySQL normalized relational databases, one to be run locally and the other to be run remotely on an Amazon AWS MySql server.
Designing the interface: we utilized the MEAN stack (excluding MongoDB) to connect and query our database as well as HTML/CSS to design our web interface. For additional interactivity, we used Plot.ly's open source graphing library to generate charts and maps.
